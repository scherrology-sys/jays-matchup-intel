The most interesting thing about Kay isn't how hard he throws. It's that he keeps his best pitch, the Changeup, almost entirely for right-handed hitters. Left-handed hitters see a completely different pitcher.
The Changeup situation
That 75% whiff rate is from one start. It will come down. But the underlying pitch is real, it has exceptional arm-side fade and drops out of the zone in a way that gets hitters to commit. Right-handed hitters in particular struggle with the arm-side run on it. The fact that he throws it 26% of the time against righties but barely at all against lefties tells you he knows exactly what it does.
The honest caveat
One start. One opponent. Some of this will look different by June. The velocity is real. The handedness split is a real tendency. The Sweeper's numbers were electric, watch for regression there. This analysis updates every time he pitches. By the next time the Jays see him, we'll have more to work with.

The edge comes from how the Changeup and the right-handed hitters in this lineup line up. Springer, Schneider, Kirk, and Guerrero all profile well against it. But the Changeup only works when the fastball is a credible threat. If Kay is missing early in counts, right-handers stop respecting the setup pitch, and the Changeup becomes much more hittable. What makes it real
The right-handed side of the Jays lineup profiles extremely well against Changeup pitchers. Kay is leading with his best pitch against that exact group. If he's confident and commanding the fastball, RHH are in for a difficult at-bat. That's a genuine matchup advantage, not flukey.
What kills it
His fastball has a high xwOBA when it misses. At 96 mph, a fastball left over the middle gets hit hard. If the command is off early, hitters stop worrying about the heater, the Changeup setup evaporates, and the edge flips. Watch the first inning ball-strike count.
Here's the short version of everything: a left-handed pitcher with a split that creates two completely different problems for left-handed and right-handed hitters. Right-handers have an edge. Left-handers are in for a tougher night.

The honest read

What we don't know yet is whether those extraordinary early numbers hold, or whether they settle back toward normal as the season goes on. We'll know a lot more after tonight.
