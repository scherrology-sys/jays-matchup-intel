What We Learned
The Jays scored two runs in the home opener of the World Series rematch. That has nothing to do with how Wrobleski pitched. He threw 5 innings and the Jays could not generate anything meaningful against him. The 14 runs the Dodgers scored came off the Jays pitching staff. The retro focuses on the half of the game we modeled: how the Jays lineup fared against Wrobleski.
The model was wrong about the Jays offense. Predicted a lineup generating above-league production against Wrobleski. They produced a team wOBA of 0.244 against him across 5 innings. MAE of 0.205, bias of +0.161. The model consistently over-predicted. The 2025 prior is too generous for this 2026 team, which is collectively underperforming its prior-season baseline by 70 points across the first seven games.

The Slider stayed (38.5%), but the setup pitch that was supposed to create the Sinker-to-Slider sequencing pattern never arrived.

In-season update verdict · first test
The blended model moved Guerrero to the top based on early-season Slider performance. He went 0-for-2 with a walk against Wrobleski. The signal was real but the sample was seven games. This is what low-confidence early-season adjustments look like when they miss. The architecture is not wrong. The confidence disclosure was accurate. One game does not invalidate the framework, but the retro has to say it missed.
The bias problem
Every 2026 game in the backtest shows positive bias: the model over-predicts the Jays offense. The 2025 prior has the team at a mean wOBA around .330. The 2026 Jays are producing around .260 through seven games, 70 points below their prior-season baseline. The in-season blend will correct this automatically as data accumulates. At seven games it is barely touching the prediction.
Three findings worth carrying forward. One about the pitcher, one about the model, one about the team.
The Sinker is not always the setup pitch
Wrobleski's career data suggested a clear Sinker-first approach to LHH. Tonight he went 4-Seam first to everyone. Whether this is a deliberate adjustment, a game-state response, or natural variation is unknown at one start. The memory system now carries a lower reliability weight for Sinker sequencing assumptions on LHP profiles of this type. The next time we see a similar pitcher the confidence interval on that component will be wider.
The prior is too generous for the 2026 Jays
The 2025 Jays were a better offensive team than the 2026 Jays have been through seven games. Eleven of twelve hitters are below their prior-season wOBA. The mean gap is 70 points. The in-season blending is the correct fix and it is already built in. It just takes more games. By May the blend will be pulling the projections meaningfully toward what the 2026 Jays are actually producing. Adjusting the prior manually based on seven games would be overfitting.
The first in-season update test came back negative
Guerrero was moved to the top of the projection based on early-season Slider performance. He produced nothing against Wrobleski. This is the correct outcome to document honestly. The mechanism is right. The signal at seven games is thin enough that misses are expected. The retro scores it as a miss, carries it into the backtest, and the cumulative evidence grows. One game in either direction does not validate or invalidate the framework.
The honest bottom line
The model thought the Jays would produce more against Wrobleski than they did. That is a bias driven by a 2025 prior that does not yet know the 2026 Jays are struggling. The Sinker assumption failed in a way that changes how the model handles similar pitcher profiles going forward. Wrobleski was better than expected. The Jays offense was worse. The 14-2 score is the pitching staff's problem. The +0.161 bias is the model's problem. Both are documented and neither is hidden.
