Cecconi went 5.2 innings, gave up 6 hits and 2 earned runs, and the lineup turned over on him in the 6th. Two innings produced all four runs. The first (Vlad RBI single, Sánchez sac fly), and the sixth (Vlad double, Sánchez 2-run HR). Bullpen held it for 2.1 innings.
What Cecconi did
90 pitches over 5.2 innings. Five-pitch arsenal: 4-Seam at 42.2%, Cutter at 30.0%, Curveball and Sinker at 10.0%, Sweeper at 7.8%. The Slider and Changeup the preview said were gone stayed gone, zero of either. Cutter usage was the structural story: he threw it 46.3% to RHH (25 of 54 right-side pitches), used it as a chase pitch with only 37% zone rate. Velocity stable across innings, 4-Seam sat 93-94 mph from the first inning to the last. First-pitch strike rate 62.5%, league average.
Vlad's two-strike masterclass
Three at-bats vs Cecconi. Three hits. All on 2-2 counts. All on different pitch types. Curveball at 68.6 mph for a line drive single in the 1st. Cutter at 88.6 mph for a ground ball single in the 3rd. 4-Seam at 94.3 mph for a sharp double to center in the 6th. He hit every offering Cecconi gave him with two strikes. The double in the 6th set up Sánchez's HR. This is the most reliable thing in the lineup right now and worth logging as a flagged positive signal for the Cecconi archetype.
The Curveball paradox
Cecconi threw 9 Curveballs total. Four of them ended at-bats. Two strikeouts (Sosa 2-2, Valenzuela 1-2). One single (Vlad). One home run (Sánchez, 3-2 hanger at 69.8 mph). Whiff rate on the pitch was 25%. Damage rate was 22%. Same pitch, opposite outcomes, depending on whose hand it landed in. This is exactly the kind of small-sample, high-leverage scenario that justifies pitch-type splits over aggregate K rates.
Sánchez and the fallback HR pattern
Sánchez sat at .320 Neutral, a fallback value because his prior-season splits did not enter the computation. He hit a 3-2 hanging Curveball over the wall in the 6th for a 2-run shot, his 4th HR of the year. This is the 4th HR in the past 7 games from a hitter the model bucketed as Neutral fallback. Cantillo had Okamoto. Williams had Okamoto and Sánchez. Cecconi has Sánchez. The fallback bucket is producing real damage at a rate that is now worth surfacing as a pattern, not just a one-off.
Lineup-level near-perfect, distribution missed
Mean exp_wOBA across the 7 scoreable hitters was 0.326. Mean actual was 0.339. Game-level MAE 0.013, the closest of the season. The model knew almost exactly how much offense the Jays would generate against Cecconi in aggregate. It did not know which hitters would do it. Vlad way over, Sánchez way over, Schneider/Okamoto/Sosa way under. This is the cleanest single-game illustration of the validation guardrail: the prediction holds at the unit it was built for (the lineup), and the hitter-level distribution remains the open frontier.
